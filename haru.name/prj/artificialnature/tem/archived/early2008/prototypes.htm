Art Installation & Research Project, Haru Ji & Graham Wakefield, 2007-2008

Abstract | Concepts | Prototypes | Implementation | Artists

Prototypes

Artificial Nature is an ongoing research project. Throughout its development, we have implmented case studies to prototype reduced portions of the entire system, to synthesize minimalized core algorithms and aesthetic approaches at each level. Some examples are outlined on this page.

 

Genetic Heredity & Rendering

An early prototype utilizing Maya and Mel scripting to evolve a population of organisms as spatial forms. Although algorithmically basic, this prototype served as our first aesthetic prototype for visual and spatial forms.

 

Gamogenesis and Propagation

Taking the organisms in abstraction, a series of prototypes produced a minimal model of 'evo-devo' interaction. This incorporates a developmental model of cell specialization according to gene structure, independent cell growth, metabolism and collapse, and an evolutionary model of genetic crossover and mutation between individuals of a population. Qualitative properties of cells derive from cell types simplified to basic functions: sensing, moving, energy exchange and reproduction.

Properties of cells (size, health, age) directly impact quantitative aspects of their behavior, and are partly environmentally, partly genotypically caused. Recognizable varieties or races diverge, however speciation is not yet possible.

 

Field Interactions & Autopoiesis

Modeling autopoietic systems progressed incrementally. The core notion of a boundary between an organizational unity (such as a cell) and an external environment was implemented first.

Individual cells move through and interact with a field of morphogens (represented by color and intensity.) Cells also maintain an inner environment of morphogen densities. Cells carry catalysts internally (represented as executable functions) to process their internal morphogens. In order to maintain themselves (and prevent death), cells exchange morphogens with the external field to maintain their requisite inner equilibrium.

Thus, according the the movement and activities of living cells, regions in the field may become gardens of eden, or toxic landscapes. Different species have different morphogen needs, leading to population explosions and collapses in complex relationships.

 

Field Dynamics and Image Generation

In order to model dissipative structures in far-from-equilibrium conditions, independent temporal processing of the morphogen field was introduced. Processing included accretion (simulating the far-from-equilibrium effect of free energy, such as the role of the sun in our own ecosystem), spatial displacement with regional variation (vital to model dissipative structures, but also suggestive of weather or surface topographies for example.)

The interactions between such simple processes and the simple cells produces complex evolving images of aesthetic interest. An important component of the installation is the mapping of user-interactions to the deformations and transformations of the regional aggregation and displacement effects upon the morphogen fields.

Agent Interaction - Sync

The phenomenon of entrainment, or sync, is used to model interaction between multiple organisms. The classic example of entrainment is the synchronized phosphorescent pulsing of fireflies. The supposed mechanism is relatively simple: each firefly has a natural pulsing period (usually quite closely matched between individuals), but indeterminate phase. Entrainment, or phase alignment, occurs by either suppressing or encouraging the likelihood of a pulse according to the amount of light energy in the vicinity of the organism. We modeled this behavior using parallel collaborative routines (coroutines) per organism, reading from and writing to a global field of luminance. Early experiments are limited to frame-rate, however current exploration extends to audio-rate parallelism in the sync periods.
